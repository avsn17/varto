Geological Context

The Karlıova Triple Junction

Varto sits at one of the most tectonically complex locations on Earth: the Karlıova Triple Junction (KTJ), where three of the world's most active fault systems converge. The Arabian Plate pushes northward at 15±2 mm/year, the Anatolian Plate moves westward at 24±2 mm/year. Convergence rate: 18–25 mm/year.

North Anatolian Fault Zone (NAFZ) — 38 segments, M7+ events
East Anatolian Fault Zone (EAFZ) — source of 2023 M7.7+7.6 earthquakes
Varto Fault Zone (VFZ) — 30 km length, right-lateral strike-slip
🌿 IGNIS DRILL ZONE — penetrates at 3,000m depth directly into this tectonic nexus

A 2025 peer-reviewed study (Bektaş et al.) analyzed Coulomb stress changes across the KTJ after the 2023 earthquakes. GNSS station BIN1 displaced 10.1 cm in the NE direction. The study warns that "major fault systems with high seismic potential could trigger a destructive earthquake in the KTJ region."

The Yedisu Seismic Gap — A Ticking Clock

The Yedisu Seismic Gap (YSG) has not produced a significant earthquake (M≥7.0) since 1784 — over 240 years of accumulated stress. Coulomb stress analysis shows positive stress lobes near the proposed JES project zone. Introducing fluid injection into this system is the equivalent of pressing on an already-compressed spring.

How Drilling Causes Earthquakes

Boron Toxicity — The Slow Death of Pastures

Boron concentrations above 1–2 mg/L become phytotoxic. In the Aegean geothermal field, boron values of 24.5 to 90.18 mg/L were measured — 15 to 90 times the toxicity threshold.

Acid Rain Over the Bingöl Mountains

H₂S released into the atmosphere forms sulfuric acid. For Varto's high-altitude alpine meadows, acid deposition would cause irreversible damage to endemic plant communities.

Thermal Pollution of the Murat River

The Varto basin feeds the Murat River, a major tributary of the Euphrates. Discharge of spent geothermal water raises stream temperatures by 2–3°C, disrupting fish reproductive cycles.
