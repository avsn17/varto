The Project

What Is Being Drilled Into the Earth?

US-based IGNIS H2 Energy Production Inc. (Texas) incorporated its Turkish subsidiary in late 2023, immediately acquiring four geothermal exploration licenses across Eastern Anatolia. The flagship Güzelkent project sits in a 40 km² zone ~12 km northwest of Varto, at the transition between the Varto Basin and the Bingöl Mountains. The company conducted Magnetotelluric (MT) geophysical surveys in 2023–2024, identifying hydrothermal reservoirs and developing a conceptual model targeting 3,000 meters depth to extract high-pressure steam for electricity generation. First drilling: May 2026.

The Muş Governorate approved drilling on 5,560 m² of communal pastureland — part of a total 453,494 m² affected area. Critically, a "No EIA Required" (ÇED Gerekli Değildir) decision was issued, bypassing Turkey's full Environmental Impact Assessment process despite the project's scope and proximity to active fault zones, biodiversity habitats, and dense rural communities.

Geological Context

The Karlıova Triple Junction

Varto sits at one of the most tectonically complex locations on Earth: the Karlıova Triple Junction (KTJ), the convergence point of three of the planet's most destructive fault systems. The Arabian Plate presses northward at 15±2 mm/year. The Anatolian Plate moves west at 24±2 mm/year relative to Eurasia. The resulting crustal shortening — 18–25 mm/year — must release somewhere. It has done so catastrophically, repeatedly, in living memory.

North Anatolian Fault Zone (NAFZ) — 38 segments, right-lateral strike-slip. Source: 1939 Erzincan M7.9 (33,000 dead), 1999 İzmit M7.6
East Anatolian Fault Zone (EAFZ) — 580 km, left-lateral, 10 mm/yr. Source: 2023 Kahramanmaraş M7.7 + M7.6 (50,000+ dead)
Varto Fault Zone (VFZ) — 30 km, right-lateral strike-slip. Three segments: Varto, Leylekdağ, Çayçatı. Source: 1966 M6.8
⚡ IGNIS drill zone — 3,000m injection into this tectonic nexus. No seismic risk study conducted.

A 2025 peer-reviewed study published in the Bulletin of Geophysics and Oceanography (Bektaş, Pırtı, Alkan, Yücel, Büyüksaraç) used GNSS satellite data from 10 continuously operating stations across the region and analyzed Coulomb stress changes from 15 local M≥5.0 earthquakes. The study found the BIN1 station — directly on the Bingöl-Karlıova segment — displaced 10.1 cm northeastward following the 2023 earthquakes, 350 km from the epicenters. This station sits on the only unbroken section of the EAFZ — a fault that has not ruptured since before records began.

The Yedisu Seismic Gap — 240 Years of Stored Energy

Between Bingöl and Karlıova lies the Yedisu Seismic Gap (YSG), approximately 70 km long and comprising the Kargapazarı, Elmalı, Yedisu, and Erzincan sub-segments of the NAFZ. This gap has not produced a significant earthquake (M≥7) since 1784 — 240+ years of accumulated elastic strain. Coulomb stress modelling shows strong positive stress lobes along all YSG segments. Seismologists predict this gap will rupture. Introducing high-pressure fluid injection near the junction where these segments begin would be one of the most reckless geological interventions imaginable.

Seismic Science

How Drilling Triggers Earthquakes

// SEISMIC SIGNAL — VARTO REGION (CONTINUOUS MONITORING)
τf = C + μ(σn − Pp)
τf = shear stress for failure  ·  C = rock cohesion  ·  μ = friction coefficient  ·  σn = normal stress  ·  Pp = pore fluid pressure
↑ INJECT FLUID AT 3,000m → ↑ Pp → ↓ (σn−Pp) → LOWER FAILURE THRESHOLD → FAULT RUPTURE

This is not theoretical. When geothermal operators reinject spent fluid underground, they raise pore pressure (Pp) inside fault planes. This mechanically lowers the friction barrier that prevents faults from slipping. The Varto Fault Zone is already at near-critical stress levels — the Coulomb stress maps from Bektaş et al. (2025) show positive stress lobes at every depth interval from 5–20 km across the KTJ. Changes of just 0.1 to 1 bar in Coulomb stress are sufficient to trigger earthquakes on prepared faults.

The Aegean Precedent — What Actually Happened

A 2022 peer-reviewed study in Environmental Science and Pollution Research (Ozcelik, Süleyman Demirel University) analyzed Turkey's largest geothermal field — the Büyük Menderes Graben with 60 plants. It found microseismic events (M<4.5) increased rapidly after 2000 as plant numbers grew, consistent with fluid-induced seismicity. However, the BMG is an extensional graben — geologically far calmer than the KTJ. The risk comparison is stark:

Chemical Hazards

What 3,000 Meters Releases

Geothermal fluids at depth are highly effective chemical solvents — they leach heavy metals, metalloids, and toxic compounds from host rocks. When brought to the surface and reinjected or spilled, these compounds enter drinking water, soils, and air. The following concentrations were actually measured at Turkey's operating Aegean geothermal plants (Ministry of Environment & Urbanization, 2020; Ozcelik, 2022):

Boron — The Silent Crop Killer

Boron is a necessary micronutrient at trace levels, but above 1–2 mg/L in irrigation water it becomes phytotoxic, inhibiting root growth and killing cereals and grasses. In the Aegean field, boron concentrations of 24.5 to 90.18 mg/L — 15 to 90 times the toxicity limit — were measured in geothermal plant fluids. These values caused documented fig, olive, and grape yield declines of 20–30%. In Varto, the affected crops are fodder grasses and pastures that sustain the entire dairy livestock economy of 22+ villages. There is no substitute crop. Contamination = economic collapse.

Acid Rain Over the Bingöl Mountains

When H₂S enters the atmosphere it reacts with moisture to form sulfuric acid — the classic acid rain mechanism. In Aydın Province (the Aegean field), air measurements averaged 7.10 µg/m³ of H₂S — exactly at the WHO 30-minute threshold for odor disturbance. For Varto's high-altitude alpine meadows, which are the last moisture refuges during increasing summer droughts and contain rare endemic flora, acid deposition causes irreversible soil acidification and loss of pH-sensitive plant communities. These meadows cannot move to safety.

The Murat River and the Euphrates

The Varto basin drains into the Murat River, a primary tributary of the Euphrates — a river system supporting agriculture across multiple countries. Discharge of geothermal effluent would introduce arsenic, boron, and heavy metals into this system, propagating contamination far beyond the project zone. The steeper topography of Varto makes surface spill containment significantly harder than the flat Aegean plains, where spills already reached rivers directly (documented by Ozcelik with photographic evidence of thermal water discharged into the Büyük Menderes).

Ecology

An Important Natural Area Under the Drill

The project zone falls within Turkey's classified Important Natural Areas (Önemli Doğa Alanı) designation — recognizing exceptional biodiversity. Botanical surveys by Muş Alparslan University documented endemic species found nowhere else. This is not generic land being developed; it is an irreplaceable ecological library being opened to a one-time industrial extraction.

🌿
Endemic Flora
Achillea schischkinii (Deli civanperçemi) and Pulicaria armena (Boz yarotu) — pollinators and traditional medicine plants unique to the undisturbed Muş-Varto corridor. Drilling roads + equipment + boron contamination = permanent habitat loss for species with no other home.
🐺
Fauna & Migration
Wolves, brown bears, raptors, and migratory birds use the thermal updrafts above volcanic plateaus. The "dağ çayırları" (mountain meadows) are last moisture refuges during summer drought. Industrial fragmentation of this landscape — even without chemical spills — destroys wildlife corridors permanently.
🌳
Saf Meşe Oak Forest
Native oak (saf meşe) forests buffer erosion, act as carbon sinks, and anchor the regional food web. Road clearing for 3,000m drilling equipment cuts through these stands. Logged stands don't recover to native forest within human lifespans — the biodiversity they support disperses or dies.
🐄
Agropastoral Commons
453,494 m² of communal grazing land (mera) is being legally reclassified for industrial use. This is not private farmland — it is collectively owned commons maintained through generations of Kurdish-Alevi transhumance. Boron contamination or physical disruption removes the foundation of 22+ villages' livelihoods simultaneously.
🌊
Euphrates Watershed
Varto's streams drain to the Murat River → Euphrates. Arsenic at 10,700 µg/L entering this system creates transboundary contamination affecting agriculture in Muş, Bingöl, and downstream provinces. Once arsenic enters alluvial aquifers, it is essentially permanent on human timescales.
🏺
Urartian Heritage
Kayalıdere fortress (20 km from Varto), excavated 1965, dates to King Sarduri II (764–735 BCE) and reveals advanced metallurgy, wine production, and temple architecture. The Varto basin contains unexcavated höyüks and necropolises. Seismic swarms from drilling cause structural fatigue in ancient stonework. No archaeological survey was required before drilling approval.

Historical Depth

A People That Has Endured Everything

Varto is not an empty landscape waiting for development. It is a place of continuous human civilization for thousands of years — shaped by volcanoes, fault lines, kingdoms, and survival. Understanding this context is inseparable from understanding why the JES project is experienced not as an energy investment, but as a final assault.

9th–7th Century BCE — Urartian Kingdom
The Varto basin serves as a strategic frontier of the Kingdom of Van. Kayalıdere reveals sophisticated bronze craft, wine cellars, and religious architecture under King Sarduri II. The volcanic plateau's fertility and its spring waters — the same hydrothermal system IGNIS now targets — sustained this civilization.
19 August 1966 — The Earthquake
2,529 people killed. Approximately 140 villages demolished across four provinces. The town of Varto is essentially erased. Survivors rebuild over decades. This is not a distant historical footnote — living people in Varto today survived this earthquake or grew up in its shadow. Their opposition to drilling is physically rooted in memory.
2000s–2010s — Systematic Ecological Pressure
HES (hydroelectric) projects begin diverting rivers. Activists call it "cutting the veins." Then quarry and mining concessions surround the mountains. The JES project arrives as the third wave. The Varto Ecology Platform names it explicitly: rivers to HES, land to JES, mountains to mines — a coordinated industrial conversion of a living landscape.
2023–2025 — Resistance Grows
IGNIS obtains its license in 2023. Communities learn of it months later — reportedly after company officials told villagers they were conducting earthquake research. By 2025, protests erupt from Varto to İzmir to Kocaeli. DEM Party and CHP submit parliamentary questions. Legal challenges filed against EIA exemption. A miting (mass rally) is announced for Varto, April 24, 2025.

Pathways Forward

What Can Stop This — Right Now

Drilling has not begun. Multiple legal, scientific, political, and community pathways exist. Each one matters. The window is open until May 2026.

01
Demand Full EIA
The "No EIA Required" decision is legally contested. Danıştay (Council of State, 6th Division) issued stay order 2022/7584 on similar exemptions. The project must be evaluated under EIA Regulation Appendix-1 Article 42 — requiring full public environmental hearings and independent scientific review before any drilling begins.
02
Independent Seismic Study
An independent assessment by international earth scientists (USGS, European Seismological Commission, KOERI) must quantify induced seismicity probability at the specific drill coordinates, with Coulomb stress modeling for the VFZ and YSG. This is standard practice for geothermal projects in seismic zones worldwide.
03
Parliamentary Pressure
DEM and CHP MPs have already submitted formal questions to two ministers. Citizens, diaspora, and European parliament supporters can amplify: constituent letters, EU parliamentary questions on human rights and environmental law, and international media attention that makes the project politically costly to advance.
04
Protect the Natural Area
File for formal legal protection of Important Natural Area (Önemli Doğa Alanı) status. Challenge the conversion of legally classified communal mera (pastureland) for private industrial use — a violation of both the Pastureland Law and the constitutional right to a healthy environment (Article 56, Turkish Constitution).
05
Community Consent (FPIC)
UN Free, Prior, and Informed Consent principles require that Varto's 22+ villages actively and genuinely consent before any project proceeds. Residents were reportedly told this was "earthquake research." This constitutes deceptive practice that invalidates any implicit consent. International human rights bodies can receive formal complaints.
06
Diaspora & International Amplification
500,000+ Vartolu live in Germany, France, Belgium, and the Netherlands. European parliaments, Turkish diaspora organizations, and international environmental NGOs can elevate this story to the international level — adding reputational and diplomatic pressure on a US company operating in Turkey without adequate safeguards.
